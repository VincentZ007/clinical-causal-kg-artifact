\begin{abstract}
Clinical notes contain many causal statements, and recent causal retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) systems use text-mined causal knowledge graphs (KGs) to ground clinical reasoning. Deciding whether to trust an extracted edge requires two checks: whether the relation exists (\textbf{existence}) and whether its orientation is correct (\textbf{direction}). We construct a directed causal KG from 328{,}839 MIMIC-IV discharge summaries using LLM extraction and UMLS normalization. We then audit three validation signals against SemMedDB as an independent reference: within-admission co-occurrence \emph{lift}, cross-admission diagnosis-coding \emph{temporal precedence}, and \emph{literature corroboration}. The audit shows a consistent asymmetry. Co-occurrence lift removes many spurious co-mention edges and is useful for existence validation. By contrast, diagnosis-coding order is a weak direction signal, reaching only 54.2\% agreement with SemMedDB, below the extractor's own 73.6\%. Literature corroboration provides independent direction evidence but covers only 30\% of edges. A cross-release replication on MIMIC-III recovers the same pattern: extractor direction remains stable (70.5\% vs.\ SemMedDB), whereas coding-order temporal direction remains near chance (50.9\%). These results suggest that text-mined clinical causal KGs should be treated as \emph{existence-reliable but direction-uncertain}. In a leakage-controlled causal QA benchmark, a direct ablation shows that grounding RAG in the lift-validated graph improves recovery of the validated cause (33.8\% vs.\ 25.9\% for the same graph without validation, $p<10^{-6}$). The gain is modest but significant and mainly reflects better recovery of the validated concept. We provide an anonymous artifact with code, aggregate summaries, and scripts to regenerate restricted outputs.

\keywords{Causal knowledge graph \and Knowledge graph validation \and Retrieval-augmented generation \and Electronic health records \and Clinical NLP.}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Mining causal relations from clinical narratives (``hepatic encephalopathy \emph{due to} alcoholic cirrhosis'') can produce causal KGs for clinical reasoning. Recent \emph{causal RAG} retrieves over text-mined causal graphs so that evidence carries direction~\cite{wang2025causalrag}. These systems must decide which extracted edges to trust. That decision has two parts: whether an edge names a real causal relation (\textbf{existence}) and whether it points in the correct direction (\textbf{direction}). An LLM reading notes can emit genuine causal edges, spurious co-mentions (``chest pain $\rightarrow$ abdominal pain''), and reversed edges. A natural validation strategy is to use structured EHR signals: keep edges whose endpoints co-occur more than expected and orient them by the order in which diagnoses appear over time. Yet these choices are rarely tested against an external reference. A spurious edge adds retrieval noise, and a misdirected edge can make a system answer ``what caused this patient's bleeding?'' with a downstream consequence. We therefore ask which of three validators, co-occurrence lift, cross-admission temporal order, and literature corroboration, can establish edge existence or direction.

We build a directed causal KG from 328K MIMIC-IV discharge summaries and audit three candidate validation signals against SemMedDB, a literature-derived causal-predicate database independent of our pipeline (Fig.~\ref{fig:overview}):
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Co-occurrence lift} validates \emph{existence}: it removes spurious co-mention edges and improves downstream causal-QA concept fidelity.
\item \textbf{Cross-admission temporal precedence} is unreliable for \emph{direction}: against literature direction it is right only 54\% of the time where it commits, worse than the extractor (74\%). ICD coding order reflects \emph{discovery}, not \emph{onset}, a hazard known to structured causal discovery~\cite{shen2021causaldiscovery} that we quantify here for text-mined edges.
\item \textbf{Literature corroboration} gives \emph{direction but only partially}: it covers 30\% of edges and agrees with the extractor's direction 74\% of the time.
\end{itemize}
Together these results expose an asymmetry: \emph{existence} can be validated with a simple EHR signal, but \emph{direction} remains uncertain. We therefore treat text-mined clinical causal KGs as \emph{existence-reliable but direction-uncertain}: validate existence with co-occurrence lift, retain the extractor's direction as a calibrated but imperfect estimate, and avoid orienting edges by coding-order time. The contribution is not a new generator architecture. It is a validation audit and reusable benchmark that asks when a causal graph mined from clinical text should be trusted before it is used for retrieval.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{A patient-grounded causal QA benchmark} with leakage-controlled, dual-signal gold answers. To our knowledge, it is the first clinical causal QA benchmark whose cause/effect answers are EHR-validated.
\item \textbf{A systematic validation audit} of three signals against independent literature evidence, yielding the \emph{existence-reliable, direction-uncertain} asymmetry. Co-occurrence lift validates existence by removing spurious edges, while cross-admission coding order is near chance and literature corroboration is coverage-limited.
\item \textbf{A reproducible causal KG construction pipeline} for 328K patient discharge notes (LLM~+~UMLS), with per-edge validation metadata (lift, temporal, SemMedDB) and a cross-release MIMIC-III replication of the audit.
\item A controlled causal-RAG study in which the unvalidated-causal system is the direct ablation of our method. Lift-validation gives a modest, significant gain in recovering the specific validated cause, with an error decomposition.
\end{enumerate}
The main value of the study is diagnostic: it identifies which validation signal can be trusted before causal edges are used for retrieval, rather than treating the QA delta as the sole contribution.

\section{Related Work}
\noindent\textbf{Clinical KGs from text and EHR.} GRAM~\cite{choi2017gram} and KAME~\cite{ma2018kame} inject ICD ontologies. Rotmensch et al.~\cite{rotmensch2017learning} learn disease-symptom graphs by co-occurrence; LLMs extract KGs from notes~\cite{nadkarni2023llmkg}; and GraphCare~\cite{jiang2024graphcare} builds per-patient KGs. These edges are usually ontological or associative, with no causal direction and no audit against patient outcomes.

\noindent\textbf{Causal KGs in medicine.} Biomedical causal KGs often draw on literature-scale semantic predications, such as SemMedDB~\cite{kilicoglu2012semmeddb}, combine literature graphs with EMR evidence~\cite{nordon2019building}, or evaluate literature-derived causal KGs in EHR case studies~\cite{lyu2023dnkg}. Others use biomedical KGs to analyze adverse drug effects~\cite{ade2026causalkg}, discover causal structure from structured variables~\cite{shen2021causaldiscovery}, or survey causal GNNs~\cite{causalgnn2025survey}, noting a ``validation crisis''~\cite{ade2026causalkg,doutreligne2024causal}. A recurring hazard is that EHR diagnosis timestamps reflect \emph{documentation} rather than \emph{onset}, so naive temporal order can mis-orient causal edges. Onset-correction methods are proposed to mitigate it~\cite{shen2021causaldiscovery}. LLMs are now used to extract causal relations from medical text~\cite{causalextract2024llm,nadkarni2023llmkg}, and SemMedDB is an established reference for evaluating biomedical causal relations~\cite{kilicoglu2012semmeddb}. None of these mine causal edges from \emph{patient narratives} and audit which validation signals recover causal structure.

\noindent\textbf{KG-augmented prediction.} GraphCare~\cite{jiang2024graphcare}, KARE~\cite{kare2025}, RAM-EHR~\cite{xu2024ramehr}, MedText~\cite{lu2021medtext}, MM-STGNN~\cite{tang2023multimodal}, and KAT-GNN~\cite{katgnn2025} couple ontology or co-occurrence graphs with EHR for outcome prediction. They do not audit validation signals.

\noindent\textbf{RAG, GraphRAG, causal RAG.} GraphRAG~\cite{edge2024graphrag}, Medical Graph RAG~\cite{medgraphrag2024}, and ontology-grounded clinical QA~\cite{llmkg2024biomed} curb hallucination by retrieving over a KG. CausalRAG~\cite{wang2025causalrag} retrieves over a \emph{causal} graph extracted from text, so the evidence carries direction. In these settings the retrieved graph is usually assumed to be correct, either because an ontology is curated or because LLM and literature edges are trusted as extracted. A spurious or misdirected edge can then propagate into the answer. We study the preceding question: which extracted edges, and which directions, should be trusted? We use an unvalidated causal-RAG variant as our reference point.

\noindent\textbf{Clinical QA benchmarks.} EHRNoteQA~\cite{kweon2024ehrnoteqa}, EHRXQA~\cite{ehrxqa2023}, EHRSQL~\cite{lee2022ehrsql}, emrQA~\cite{pampari2018emrqa} target factual/table questions; general-domain causal reasoning QA exists~\cite{yang2022causalreasoning} but is not clinical or patient-grounded. None pair \emph{causal} questions with EHR-validated gold answers. We contribute one.

\section{Building the Causal KG}
\noindent\textbf{Extraction.} A discharge summary mixes a free-text narrative with templated patient-instruction boilerplate (``call your doctor if\ldots''); the latter repeats across notes and otherwise dominates raw edge frequency. We therefore restrict extraction to the History of Present Illness and Hospital Course sections (including ``Brief Hospital Course''), and prompt Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct~\cite{qwen2025} (AWQ, vLLM~\cite{kwon2023vllm}, temperature~0) to emit a JSON array of \texttt{\{"cause","effect"\}} pairs per note. Over the 328{,}839 MIMIC-IV-Note discharge summaries~\cite{johnson2023mimicnote}, 79.5\% yield $\geq$1 triple, giving 1.31M triples (3.98 per note) and 1.17M unique surface edges. The unique-edge distribution is heavy-tailed: 96.4\% occur exactly once. Because the singleton tail is dominated by hyper-specific or noisy spans, we keep only edges attested in $\geq$2 notes (42{,}798), retaining the singletons as per-note provenance for benchmark grounding.

\noindent\textbf{Normalization.} Surface phrases are noisy (synonyms, abbreviations, modifiers), so we link each cause/effect span to a UMLS~\cite{bodenreider2004umls} Concept Unique Identifier (CUI) with a scispaCy~\cite{neumann2019scispacy} candidate generator, accepting the top candidate when cosine similarity $\geq0.80$ and its semantic types are not in a non-clinical blocklist; co-referring surface forms thereby collapse to one concept node. Linking raises the giant connected component from 86.9\% to 97.1\% and more than doubles the link rate versus a rule-based cue-phrase extractor (49.3\% vs.\ 21.9\%) at comparable graph size (Table~\ref{tab:kg}), confirming that LLM spans are cleaner concepts. Table~\ref{tab:kg} reports the full pre-split graph. After a patient-level split for leakage control (\S\ref{sec:qa}), the train-only KG used for retrieval and release has 3{,}899 concept nodes and 16{,}273 directed edges.

\section{The Validation Audit}\label{sec:audit}
We score three candidate signals against two questions: whether an edge \emph{exists} and which \emph{direction} it points. As an independent reference we use SemMedDB v43~\cite{kilicoglu2012semmeddb}, a repository of 115.5M semantic predications extracted from PubMed. We keep the causal predicates \textsc{causes}, \textsc{predisposes}, and \textsc{induces}. We read the subject$\rightarrow$object orientation as the literature's causal direction and restrict the analysis to predications whose subject and object CUIs both occur in our KG. When both orientations are attested, we take the majority by citation support. This yields literature causal evidence for 4{,}943 KG edges. SemMedDB is imperfect (relaxed $F_1\approx0.5$~\cite{kilicoglu2012semmeddb}), so we treat it as an independent reference rather than ground truth. We also compare each conclusion with the extractor's own text-derived direction.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Experimental logic.} The audit and the downstream QA study answer different questions. The audit asks which signal can answer which trust question. Each signal is reported by coverage and, when it commits to a direction, agreement with SemMedDB. The QA study then asks whether the reliable part of the audit, existence filtering by lift, helps causal RAG. All QA systems share the same generator, prompt, and retrieval budget, so differences isolate the retrieved evidence rather than the surrounding model setup.

\subsection{Co-occurrence lift removes spurious edges}
We map ICD diagnoses to CUIs and, for edge $A\rightarrow B$, compute the within-admission $\mathrm{lift}=P(B\mid A)/P(B)$. An edge is treated as \emph{supported} when $\mathrm{lift}>1$ with sufficient co-occurrence; 2{,}047 LLM edges meet this criterion. Lift is \emph{symmetric}, so it measures association strength rather than direction. It demotes co-mention noise: ``chest pain $\rightarrow$ abdominal pain'' (two common symptoms with high base rates) is not supported ($\mathrm{lift}\approx1$), whereas ``alcoholic cirrhosis $\rightarrow$ hepatic encephalopathy'' is strongly supported ($\mathrm{lift}=65$). Section~\ref{sec:qa} shows that retrieving over the lift-supported subgraph improves downstream causal-QA concept fidelity. Co-occurrence lift is therefore useful for existence validation, not orientation.

\subsection{Cross-admission temporal precedence does not recover direction}
We hypothesized that across admissions a cause is coded \emph{before} its effect. We test this: for the 4{,}943 edges with SemMedDB direction, compare the extractor's surface direction and temporal precedence against the literature gold (Table~\ref{tab:audit}).

\noindent\emph{Interpretation.} ICD coding order reflects \emph{discovery/coding}, not \emph{onset}: chronic conditions are co-coded, and findings are coded at the encounter in which they are discovered. This timestamp hazard is known to structured causal discovery, where onset-correction (distinguishing incident from prevalent diagnoses) is the proposed remedy~\cite{shen2021causaldiscovery}. Here we quantify the same hazard for text-mined causal-edge validation, where the temporal signal is barely above chance and below the extractor's own direction. SemMedDB itself has direction noise, but it and the extractor are both text-derived and agree 74\%, while the structured temporal signal correlates with neither. Applying onset-correction to the temporal signal is left to future work.

\noindent\textbf{Robustness.} The McNemar comparison is paired over all 4{,}943 SemMedDB-covered edges. Each edge has an extractor verdict, and the temporal signal is counted as correct only if it commits to the literature direction. Abstentions are therefore failures for an independent directioner. The committed-only accuracy in Table~\ref{tab:audit} is a diagnostic subset, not the McNemar denominator. The gap is unlikely to be a SemMedDB artifact. The extractor and SemMedDB are produced by independent text-mining pipelines yet agree on 74\% of directions, whereas the structured temporal signal agrees with neither. This is the pattern expected if diagnosis-coding order carries little causal-direction information.

\subsection{Literature corroboration is useful but partial}
Among the 2{,}047 lift-supported edges, 886 (43\%) are also attested by SemMedDB. The attested subset agrees with the extractor's direction 74\% of the time and covers 61\% of benchmark gold relations. Its value depends on how it is used. As a strict filter requiring both EHR-lift support and literature attestation, it would discard the remaining 1{,}161 lift-supported EHR edges (57\%) that fall outside SemMedDB's PubMed-derived coverage. This would hurt recall. As a confidence tier, it separates high-confidence edges (lift \emph{and} literature) from EHR-only edges and supplies an independent direction for the subset it covers. We therefore use it as coverage-limited corroboration rather than as a strict filter.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Takeaway.} Co-occurrence lift supports \emph{existence}; \emph{direction} remains uncertain because coding-order time is near chance and literature covers a minority of edges. Text-mined clinical causal edges are therefore best treated as existence-reliable but direction-uncertain. We validate existence with lift, keep the extractor's direction as an uncertain estimate, and avoid orienting edges by coding-order time.

\subsection{Putting it together: a validation recipe}
The audit yields a reproducible recipe for converting raw LLM-extracted causal edges into a validated graph, without manual curation (Fig.~\ref{fig:funnel}). Starting from 42{,}798 recurrent surface edges, we first normalize endpoints to UMLS concepts (\S\ref{sec:audit} reduces 16{,}151 surface nodes to 4{,}318 concepts). We then keep edges with within-admission lift~$>1$, yielding 2{,}047 supported edges, and orient each edge by the extractor's direction rather than by coding-order time. Finally, we flag the 886 literature-corroborated edges as a high-confidence core and treat the remainder as EHR-only. The resulting graph has \emph{existence} claims grounded in both text and structured co-occurrence. Its \emph{direction} claims retain source-aware uncertainty. Section~\ref{sec:qa} shows that this graph improves downstream causal QA, and the recipe transfers to note corpora paired with linked structured records.

\subsection{Cross-release replication on MIMIC-III}
To test whether the audit depends on the MIMIC-IV release, we reran the same extraction, UMLS normalization, structured-diagnosis lift validation, temporal analysis, and SemMedDB comparison on MIMIC-III. The replication uses 53{,}981 usable discharge-summary sections and yields 237{,}957 clean triples, 221{,}454 surface edges, and 35{,}232 CUI-level edges. Although this graph is larger and noisier than the MIMIC-IV train graph, the qualitative conclusions replicate (Fig.~\ref{fig:mimic3}): 942 of 2{,}318 diagnosis-testable edges are lift-supported (40.6\%); SemMedDB covers 6{,}753 edges (19.2\%); extractor direction remains aligned with SemMedDB (70.5\%, close to 73.6\% on MIMIC-IV); and coding-order direction remains near chance (50.9\%, close to 54.2\%). Thus MIMIC-III strengthens the central audit claim under a different EHR release: existence validation transfers, while naive temporal orientation remains unreliable.

\section{Causal QA Benchmark and Downstream Utility}\label{sec:qa}
\subsection{Benchmark}
A causal relation becomes a gold answer only under a \emph{dual signal}. It must be stated as cause/effect by the treating clinician in a \emph{held-out} note, and it must be EHR-lift-supported ($\mathrm{lift}\geq2$). This pairing removes symptom co-mention noise that either signal alone would admit. We split the 145{,}872 patients of MIMIC-IV~\cite{johnson2023mimiciv} 85/15 by \texttt{subject\_id} (124{,}058 train / 21{,}814 test). The KG's edges are built from \emph{train} notes only, so no test patient's note contributes an edge. Population-level lift may use all patients' codes, a different modality that cannot reveal an individual's text answer. Questions are drawn from \emph{test} patients and answered against the train-only KG.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Statistics.} The benchmark pool contains 2{,}319 patient-grounded items over WHY, WHATCAUSES, and direction-discrimination templates. The downstream experiment evaluates the forward causal-QA subset: WHY (``most likely underlying cause of \{effect\}?'') and WHATCAUSES (``what does \{cause\} most likely lead to?''), with 466 items each (932 total) over 170 validated causal edges. Each question is presented with the patient's coded problem list. To avoid a trivially solvable task, in $\sim$half of items the gold cause is not already in the coded problem list. The system must supply it from causal knowledge rather than copy it from the list. A balanced 120-item subset is human-checked for answer correctness and direction. Correctness means that the predicted concept CUI-matches the reference, or that a Qwen judge confirms the same condition. Faithfulness means that the predicted concept appears in the system's retrieved evidence.

\subsection{Does validation help causal RAG?}
\noindent\textbf{Why these baselines.} No published system exactly matches patient-grounded causal QA over an EHR-mined causal graph under this validation setting. Rather than compare against systems that also change the generator, chunking, or prompt, we re-implement six retrieval paradigms under one harness. All systems share the same generator (Qwen2.5-7B), prompt, and retrieval budget, and differ \emph{only} in the retrieved evidence: (1)~\textbf{closed-book}, none; (2)~\textbf{text-RAG}, top-3 TF-IDF discharge-note excerpts; (3)~\textbf{SemMedDB-only}, a directed literature causal graph, standing for external biomedical-KG grounding~\cite{kilicoglu2012semmeddb}; (4)~\textbf{assoc-graph}, undirected co-occurrence neighbours, the GraphRAG / associative-graph style~\cite{edge2024graphrag,medgraphrag2024,jiang2024graphcare}; (5)~\textbf{unvalidated-causal}, the raw LLM directed graph with no validation, a CausalRAG-style system that trusts extracted edges~\cite{wang2025causalrag}; and (6)~\textbf{lift-validated} (ours), the lift-supported directed graph. Thus any difference isolates the retrieved knowledge (Table~\ref{tab:qa}).
The unvalidated-causal condition is the direct ablation of our method: the same extracted graph with the validation step removed.

For the graph systems, retrieval is direction-aware: a WHY question pulls the \emph{in}-edges of the asked effect (its candidate causes) and a WHATCAUSES question its \emph{out}-edges, ranked by lift, frequency, or PMID support. The top~12 are serialized into the prompt, and the lift-validated system additionally annotates each candidate with its lift. The generator returns a single condition, which we then match. Generation and judging run on a 7B open model on a private cluster, so MIMIC text does not leave the compliant environment.

\subsection{Analysis of the gain}
This experiment probes the \emph{existence}-validation part of the audit. The questions are forward, so direction is fixed, and any gain should come from lift removing spurious causes. The lift-validated graph recovers the specific validated cause $+7.9$ pts more often than the unvalidated graph. Decomposing the paired wins with the judge shows that the gain is mostly \emph{concept specificity}: the unvalidated system often names a clinically valid sibling, such as ``Alcoholic Cirrhosis'' rather than the reference ``Liver Cirrhosis, Alcoholic''. Thus lift-validation mainly improves \emph{concept fidelity}, surfacing the canonical validated concept rather than preventing many broad clinical errors. We also find that naive text-RAG underperforms closed-book generation, consistent with distraction from noisy retrieved text, while SemMedDB-only retrieval improves over text-RAG but remains coverage-limited.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Validation does not trade away recall.} Filtering edges by lift could remove correct answers. In this benchmark it does not: the gold answer is present in the retrieved candidate set for 77.8\% of items under lift-validated retrieval, versus 72.3\% for the unvalidated graph, 72.7\% for the associative graph, and 23.9\% for SemMedDB-only retrieval. The lift filter's cleaner ranking surfaces the true cause within the top candidates more often. The generator therefore sees the answer more frequently and is less distracted by spurious neighbours, explaining the concept-fidelity gain.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{By question type.} WHATCAUSES is harder than WHY for every system (22.3\% vs.\ 45.3\% for ours). This follows from its one-to-many nature: a cause has many clinically valid effects, so matching a single reference effect undercounts all systems. The ordering of systems is preserved within each type, so the paired comparison remains valid.

\smallskip\noindent\textbf{Error analysis.} Three modes account for most residual errors. First, the system may name a clinically valid sibling of the reference concept and be marked wrong by exact-concept matching. This is the largest class. Second, retrieval gaps remain: for $\sim$22\% of items no graph surfaces the gold cause among its top candidates, capping achievable accuracy. Third, text-RAG and, to a lesser degree, the unvalidated graph sometimes answer a salient but wrong concept drawn from a noisy evidence list. This explains why text-RAG falls below the no-retrieval baseline.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}
\noindent\textbf{Practical recommendations.} For practitioners building causal KGs or causal RAG from clinical text, the audit yields three recommendations. First, filter edges by within-admission co-occurrence lift to remove spurious co-mentions before using them for retrieval. Second, avoid orienting edges by EHR diagnosis-coding order, which is near chance and below the extractor's own direction. Third, use literature corroboration (e.g.\ SemMedDB) as a confidence tier, while recognizing that it covers only a fraction of edges.

\noindent\textbf{On the temporal hazard.} That coding timestamps reflect documentation rather than onset is known to structured causal discovery, where onset-correction is the standard remedy~\cite{shen2021causaldiscovery}. Our contribution is to quantify, against an independent literature reference, how badly the \emph{naive} signal fails specifically when used to orient text-mined edges; applying onset-correction here is a natural next step and may recover part of the signal. SemMedDB has its own direction noise, so we do not treat it as perfect. We report agreement against two independent text-derived directions (the extractor and the literature), which agree 74\%, while the structured temporal signal agrees with neither.

\noindent\textbf{Threats to validity.} Our gold relations are EHR-validated and therefore overlap the lift-validated graph, which could in principle favour it. The unvalidated baseline, however, contains a \emph{superset} of those edges. The comparison therefore isolates the effect of removing noise rather than the effect of possessing the answers. The measured gain is precision, specifically concept fidelity, which is consistent with this design. The correctness metric rewards recovery of the canonical concept. Under a lenient clinical-acceptability metric the systems converge, which we report as a boundary of the benchmark.

\noindent\textbf{Other limitations.} We use a single 7B open model and English ICU notes. The downstream QA benchmark is built on MIMIC-IV; MIMIC-III is used to test whether the validation signals replicate across an older EHR release, not to duplicate the full QA benchmark. This choice keeps the external experiment aligned with the paper's central audit question. Absolute QA numbers are modest, but the relative differences are statistically significant. Lift covers diagnosis-coded endpoints, so causes involving drugs, adverse events, and clinical findings are presently out of scope; integrating prescription and adverse-event signals, as well as multi-hop causal chains, would extend coverage. SemMedDB corroborates only a minority of edges, limiting literature-based direction to that subset.

\noindent\textbf{Released artifacts.} To support reproducible causal-KG construction from clinical text, the anonymous artifact package at \url{https://anonymous.4open.science/r/clinical-causal-kg-artifact-C950/} provides all extraction, validation, and evaluation code, the paper source and figures, non-patient-level aggregate summaries, and the MIMIC-III replication summary. Because MIMIC, UMLS, and SemMedDB restrict redistribution, we do not upload raw notes, patient-grounded benchmark files, model prompts or outputs derived from notes, or raw literature-predication tables. The scripts regenerate the validated KG, benchmark instances, and audit labels once the reviewer has credentialed access to the required resources.

\section{Conclusion}
Text-mined clinical causal KGs require validation at two levels: relation existence and edge direction. Auditing three EHR/literature signals against independent literature evidence, we find that co-occurrence lift supports existence validation, whereas cross-admission coding order is unreliable for direction. Literature corroboration helps with direction but covers only a minority of edges. The MIMIC-III replication recovers the same qualitative pattern, suggesting that the conclusion is not an artifact of a single MIMIC release. Text-mined clinical causal KGs are therefore best treated as existence-reliable but direction-uncertain. In a controlled causal-RAG ablation, grounding retrieval in the lift-validated graph modestly but significantly improves recovery of the specific validated cause, mainly by improving concept fidelity. More broadly, the results show why validation signals should be audited against an independent reference before being used to trust or orient causal edges. The same recipe can be applied to other note corpora and to richer validators, including onset-corrected timing, prescription signals, and multi-hop causal chains.

\bigskip\noindent\textbf{Additional QA decomposition.} A variant that adds temporal orientation to the lift-validated graph reaches 35.3\% overall. Relative to the unvalidated graph (25.9\%), the lift filter contributes $+7.9$ points and temporal orientation contributes only $+1.5$ points. This is consistent with the audit in \S\ref{sec:audit}, where temporal orientation is unreliable against independent literature evidence. Win decomposition for lift-validated vs.\ unvalidated retrieval ($n{=}932$): both correct 164, both wrong 525, only-validated 166, and only-unvalidated 77. Of the wins, the net gain is $+4$ substantive cases (clinically wrong answers prevented) and $+85$ specificity cases (canonical-concept recovery). The QA gain is therefore mainly concept fidelity rather than clinical-error prevention.
